My Journey: From Syntax to Superpowers

Anshul Namdev 2026-01-13 5 min read personal, career, learning, advice

It was back in 6th grade when I first got introduced to the world of programming. Now, looking back after 6-7 years, it feels like a lifetime of discovery. From those early days of curiosity to where I am now, my journey has been a rollercoaster of syntax errors, "aha!" moments, and a profound shift in how I view technology.

Over the years, I've dabbled in a multitude of languages: Java, Python, JS, Bash, Ruby, and Rust. Each brought its own flavor and philosophy, but looking back, I realize I fell into a common trap that many beginners face.

The "Syntax Trap"

I was learning languages fast. Too fast. I would jump from one language to another, mastering the basic syntax, learning a few functions, and then feeling like I had "learned" the language. But I was missing the point.

Programming languages are not just about syntax. It's easy to think that knowing how to reverse an array in Rust means you know Rust. But that's just control. Real mastery is having command over the language, knowing how to build something tangible, something useful.

"Knowing how to reverse an array but not being able to build something using that language is a waste of DSA and potential."

I spent a lot of time on DSA (Data Structures and Algorithms). Don't get me wrong, DSA is important, but practical implementation is where the real magic happens. Investing too much time in abstract problems without building real-world projects can leave you feeling stuck in the terminal, unable to affect the world outside it.

Entering the Realm of Libraries

The turning point for me was realizing that we need to stop at the syntax and basic functions and enter the realm of libraries. These are the superpowers. They are what allow you to move outside the terminal and interact with the real world, building web servers, automating tasks, creating games, and analyzing data.

Once I embraced libraries and frameworks, I felt a massive unlock in my potential. I wasn't just solving puzzles anymore; I was creating solutions.

Why I Love Programming

Programming has fundamentally changed how I look at problems. It's taught me patience, logic, and the art of breaking down complex issues into manageable chunks. And yes, while I made the mistake of jumping between languages too quickly in the past, today I feel that diverse exposure has made me a better engineer.

I feel confident now. I feel skilled enough to know that I won't be easily replaced by an AI-governed environment. AI is a tool, but the creativity, the architectural thinking, and the human insight we bring to code is irreplaceable.

Advice for Fellow Programmers

  • Don't just learn syntax. Build things. Break things. Fix things.
  • Master one language deeply before jumping to the next.
  • Use libraries. They are your best friends.
  • Balance DSA with Development. You need both to be a complete engineer.

Thank you for reading my story. Keep coding, keep building!
